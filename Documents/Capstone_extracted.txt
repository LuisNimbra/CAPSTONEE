Traditional job portals often only list or store market labor information, which can make it difficult to identify meaningful matches. In contrast, advanced job matching systems use automated and analytical methods to more effectively connect job seekers with suitable opportunities, helping to overcome information gaps and inefficiencies in referral processes (World Bank, 2023).Research also highlights the importance of intelligent skill mapping and structured job matching systems as a key mechanism for improving labor market alignment. Data-driven matching systems, which analyze individual skill profiles and educational backgrounds, significantly reduce education–employment mismatches by recommending jobs and training pathways that best fit a person’s current competencies and career aspirations (Sanguino et al. 2025).With advancements in artificial intelligence (AI), employment and career development systems are increasingly incorporating AI techniques to enhance skill assessment, job recommendation, and career guidance. A systematic review showing that AI tools are transforming workplace skill profiles by identifying emerging skill demands and enabling data-driven decision-making in talent alignment and job placements across industry sectors (Babashahi et al. 2024). Research underscores the role of technology in career guidance systems that support continuous career development. Research found that although many existing systems excel at facilitating job placement, systems often lack integrated features for long-term career planning, skill gap analysis, and personalized guidance — areas that are critical for empowering individuals to navigate evolving careers over time (Bankins et al. 2024). In the local context of City of San Jose del Monte (CSJDM), the Public Employment Service Office (PESO) continues to experience limitations in skill profiling accuracy, referral efficiency, and career guidance delivery. The current process relies heavily on manual screening and matching of applicants to job vacancies, which becomes inefficient as the volume of applicants increases, leading to delays, inconsistent referrals, and possible oversight of qualified candidates. Based on the reviewed literature, there remains a clear gap in implementing a unified, machine learning-driven information system that integrates skill mapping, mediated job referral, and career guidance tailored for localized government employment services. Addressing this gap offers the potential to improve PESO CSJDM’s ability to provide responsive, data-driven, and sustainable employment support that aligns skills with real job opportunities and fosters long-term career success. To address these gaps, this study proposes the development of a Web-Based Supervised Machine Learning-Driven Skill Mapping and Job Referral Decision Support System for PESO. The system aims to improve the accuracy of skill-to-job matching, enhance the efficiency of job referrals, and provide data-driven career guidance to job seekers. By integrating machine learning-driven information systems, the proposed system seeks to support PESO CSJDM in delivering more effective, responsive, and data-informed employment services.Objectives of the StudyThe primary objective of this study is to design and develop a Web-Based Supervised Machine Learning-Driven that integrates skill mapping, mediated job referral, and career guidance to improve the effectiveness of employment services provided by the PESO CSJDM. Specifically, this study aims:To design and develop a Web-Based Supervised Machine Learning-Driven Skill Mapping and Job Referral Decision Support System for PESO. To integrate a Machine Learning-driven Job Matching module to predict the most suitable job titles, an Explanation Module to provide an organized visual summary of applicant suitability, and an Analytical Dashboard to support PESO staff in delivering personalized career guidance. Evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed system based on ISO 25010 software quality standards, particularly in terms of functional suitability, usability, and performance efficiency, in supporting PESO CSJDM’s employment facilitation and career development services. Significance of the StudyThis study is significant to various stakeholders as it addresses the growing need for data-driven and intelligent employment services, particularly within local government units.PESO CSJDMThe system offers a modernized and efficient approach to employment facilitation. By integrating Supervised Machine Learning-enhanced skill mapping and mediated job referral, the office can improve the accuracy of job matching, reduce manual workload, and provide more responsive career guidance services. This supports PESO CSJDM’s mandate to deliver inclusive and sustainable employment assistance to the local workforce.Job SeekersStand to benefit from more accurate job recommendations and personalized career guidance. This empowers individuals to make informed career decisions and increases their chances of securing suitable employment.EmployersBenefit from better-matched job referrals, as the system improves the alignment between job requirements and applicant competencies. The system will be tested by PESO staff in the city through simple user testing and surveys to check how easy it is to use and how helpful it is. LimitationsThis project focuses only on the design and implementation of a Web-Based Supervised Machine Learning-Driven Skill Mapping and Job Referral Decision Support System for PESO. The system focuses on processing data uploaded by PESO and will not collect data directly to employers and job seekers. As well as the system does not process unstructured data such as resume or any free text format file. The system does not entirely automate the hiring process; final decisions are made by PESO workers and employers. The accuracy of the data submitted by users is essential to the ML-based matching and prediction findings, and wrong information can have an impact on the outcomes. The study also does not assess long-term employment outcomes, such as job retention or career advancement, following the referral procedure.  Definition of TermsArtificial Intelligence (AI)Refers to the use of computer-based techniques that enable the system to analyze data, identify patterns, and generate recommendations related to skill mapping, job matching, and career guidance.AI-Enhanced Skill MappingThe process of using artificial intelligence to analyze a job seeker’s skills, education, training, and work experience in order to create a structured and accurate skill profile for employment matching.Applicant Scoringand RankingA system feature that assigns weighted scores to job seekers based on their qualifications, skills, and experience, and ranks them according to their suitability for specific job opportunities. Career GuidanceRefers      to    system-generated recommendations and insights that help job seekers understand possible career paths, identify skill gaps, and make informed decisions   about   employment and skill development.Career Pathway RecommendationA feature that suggests potential career directions or job roles based on a job seeker’s current skills, experience, and employment trends identified by the system.Job MatchingThe process of aligning a job seeker’s skill profile with employer job requirements using AI algorithms to identify suitable employment opportunities.Job ReferralThe process by which qualified job seekers are endorsed or recommended by PESO CSJDM to employers based on system-generated matching results.Labor Market RecommendationData related to job demand, required skills, and employment opportunities that are used by the system to support accurate job matching and career guidance.Machine LearningBranch of artificial intelligence that enables computers to learn from data and improve their performance without being explicitly programmed. Data was acquired by the researcher from reliable websites and journal publications. Review of Related LiteraturePublic Employment Service Offices (PESO) in the Philippines play a critical role in connecting job seekers with potential employers. Local PESOs, such as those in Davao de Oro, implement job matching programs to link applicants to suitable job vacancies, helping to reduce unemployment and improve access to employment opportunities. While these programs demonstrate the effectiveness of PESO in facilitating employment, the existing systems rely heavily on manual review and intervention by PESO staff, and the matching process is primarily based on basic profile and qualification comparison. Current platforms, including the PESO Employment Information System (PEIS), function as centralized databases that store applicant and employer information but do not provide advanced decision support features, such as automated ranking, predictive analytics, or intelligent job recommendations. Therefore, there is a clear research gap in the development of decision support systems tailored for PESO operations, which could enhance efficiency, provide analytical insights, and assist staff in making data-driven employment facilitation decisions (Michael L. Uy. 2025 and Public Employment Service Office, n.d.).Research on automated job‑matching systems frequently applies natural language processing (NLP) based semantic similarity techniques to interpret the meaning behind resumes and job descriptions but stops short of using supervised machine learning models trained on actual hiring outcomes. For example, a recent study introduced a real‑time recruitment framework that integrates semantic similarity with NLP tools such as TF‑IDF vectorization and cosine similarity scoring to compute conceptual relevance between candidate resumes and job postings, improving upon traditional keyword‑based matching by capturing contextual meaning in text. However, this approach remains focused on similarity scoring between unstructured text rather than predicting the probability of successful placement or using historical decision data to inform recommendations, which limits its capacity as a true decision support tool. In contrast, the proposed research develops a supervised machine learning–driven decision support system that goes beyond text similarity to learn from labeled placement outcomes, making it better suited for supporting employment decisions in public employment services. This highlights a gap in the literature where ML for job matching has mostly centered on semantic similarity rather than predictive outcome‑oriented learning (Ajjam, M.‑H., & Al‑Raweshidy, H. S. 2025).Many existing job matching and recommendation systems rely on similaritybased vector techniques to align candidate profiles with job descriptions. In such systems, textual content from resumes and job posts is preprocessed and transformed into numerical vectors using techniques like Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency (TFIDF), and cosine similarity is computed between these vectors to assess how closely a candidate’s skills and experiences match job requirements. Candidates are then ranked based on their similarity scores, with higher scores indicating better matches. While effective for straightforward matching, this method primarily captures surfacelevel alignment and may not fully represent deeper or complex relationships between candidate competencies and job needs (Kotha et al., 2025).Despite the increasing use of machine learning in job recommendation systems, many models operate as opaque “black box” systems that do not provide meaningful explanations for their predictions. This lack of explainability hinders users’ ability to understand, trust, and evaluate the recommendations produced, particularly in highstakes contexts such as employment decisions. A systematic review of person–job recommendation systems found that most existing approaches focus on optimizing predictive accuracy but fail to offer transparent reasoning or interpretable outputs, highlighting explainability as an ongoing research challenge that must be addressed to improve trust, fairness, and user acceptance (Tang et al., 2025).Many existing job recommendation systems are developed primarily to provide direct recommendations to job seekers rather than to assist intermediaries such as employment facilitators or human resource personnel in mediated job referral decisions. For example, one study proposed a job recommender based on job profile clustering and job seeker behavior, where job postings are collected and clustered, and job seekers receive highly personalized job recommendations according to their interactions and preferences. This approach focuses on enhancing the job seeker’s experience and efficiency in finding jobs, without incorporating mechanisms that support decisionmaking by employment facilitators or organizational recruiters. The lack of mediated support thus limits the applicability of such systems in institutional contexts where intermediaries evaluate and endorse candidate matches (Job Recommendation based on Job Profile (D. Mhamdi2020).Improving Labor Market Information Systems (LMIS) is key to reducing skills mismatches and ensuring that workers' skills align with industry needs. Bayudan-Dacuycuy et al. (2024) looked at the Philippine LMIS and Technical Education and Skills Development Authority (TESDA) skills anticipation framework. Certain challenges are found, including scattered labor data, a lack of a standardized skills taxonomy, and poor coordination among platforms. These issues limit the effectiveness of job matching, workforce planning, and employment support services. The study highlights that well-structured and integrated labor market data is essential for predicting skill demand and offering useful career guidance. The findings also show that reliable datasets and organized systems can help develop predictive tools and data-driven algorithms. These improvements enhance job matching and generate targeted career advice. Overall, the research supports the need for systems that better link workforce skills with labor market needs. This connection can boost employment support and improve long-term workforce planning.In order to determine whether the competencies available in the community workforce align with the skills required by local industries, this study carried out a labor market assessment (Bacay et al., 2025). The study looked at present and future skill needs in industries like skilled labor and engineering using a combination of quantitative data and qualitative focus group discussions. It discovered a persistent mismatch between workforce skills and job requirements (Bacay et al., 2025). The authors support the justification for systems that incorporate labor market insights into job matching and skills gap analysis by recommending focused skills development programs, increased cooperation between training providers and industry partners, and continuous labor market monitoring mechanisms. The study presents a learning-based matched representation system that aims to improve the accuracy of job recommendations (Alsaif et al., 2022). Traditional job matching platforms often rely on simple keyword matching, which fails to capture the deeper relationship between a job seeker’s skills and job requirements. To address the limitation, the study proposes a content-based filtering approach that extracts and compares skills from resumes and job descriptions using text similarity measures such as cosine similarity and the Jaccard coefficient. The method enables more precise alignment between skills and job opportunities, especially in large-scale datasets. Hakan Gülten and Hayri Baraçlı (2024) argue that when you spot potential leaders early on with a decision support model, you can actually build up skills through hands-on training and real-world experience as they move through their careers. They also point out that by digging into complex big data with advanced methods, it’s possible to come up with smarter, more effective ways to find future leaders. People have started using intelligent job matching models beyond just commercial platforms. One team used a content-based filtering model to recommend jobs to vocational education graduates. Machine learning steered these job seekers toward careers that actually fit their skills and backgrounds. The researchers worked with structured frameworks like CRISP-DM for data mining and similarity checks. The results show that smart matching systems really do help graduates find jobs that suit them, making the whole job search smoother and offering better guidance for planning their next steps (Farell et al., 2024). Review of Related SystemsBERT Embeddings SystemDeveloped by Aram Khasro Jirjees et al. (2025), is a machine learning-based recruitment system designed to improve job-applicant matching accuracy. The system addresses the limitations of traditional keyword-based recruitment methods by using Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT) embeddings to capture contextual meaning from job descriptions and candidate profiles (Aram Khasro Jirjees et al. Results showed that BERT and SVM achieved 94% accuracy, while LSTM reached 92% and MLP achieved 90%, demonstrating improved contextual job matching performance. Similar to the proposed system, the study utilizes supervised machine learning to enhance recruitment accuracy. However, it mainly focuses on job title classification using large-scale datasets and does not implement a localized web-based decision support system tailored for a government employment office such as the Public Employment Service Office in the City of San Jose del Monte.Job Recommendation System Model (JRS MODEL) Developed by Suleiman Ali Alsaif et al. Job postings were collected from Indeed across major cities in Saudi Arabia, and similarity between resumes and job descriptions was computed using Jaccard Coefficient and Cosine Similarity (Suleiman Ali Alsaif et al. The system was evaluated using decision support metrics such as precision, recall, and F1-score. Similar to the proposed system, it applies machine learning and natural language processing techniques to improve job matching accuracy. However, the system focuses on a general online job recommendation framework and does not implement a localized, government-integrated, web-based supervised machine learning decision support system specifically designed for a Public Employment Service Office such as the Public Employment Service Office of the City of San Jose del Monte. Labor Market Matching SystemProposed by Sabrina Mühlbauer and Enzo Weber (2021) applies supervised machine learning techniques to improve the matching of job seekers with available vacancies. Various machine learning models were evaluated using measures such as predictive accuracy and overall matching performance, demonstrating that data-driven approaches provide more precise and efficient matching results compared to conventional methods. Similar to the proposed study, the system utilizes supervised machine learning to enhance employment decision-makingHowever, the system focuses on large-scale labor market data analysis and institutional-level optimization rather than developing a localized, web-based skill mapping and job referral decision support system specifically intended for a government employment office such as the Public Employment Service Office of the City of San Jose del Monte.NLP-Based Bi-Directional Recommendation SystemDeveloped by Suleiman Ali Alsaif et al. (2022), the system was created to improve the job matching process between job seekers and recruiters through the use of natural language processing and machine learning. Instead of relying only on simple keyword searches, the system analyzes the actual content of resumes and job descriptions to better understand skills, qualifications, and job requirements. It uses techniques such as Named Entity Recognition (NER) and similarity computation to recommend suitable jobs to applicants while also suggesting potential candidates to employers. The study evaluated the system using standard performance measures like accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score, and the results showed that it performed well, even when comparing job roles with similar or overlapping skills (Alsaif et al., 2022). In comparison to our proposed Web-Based Supervised Machine Learning-Driven Skill Mapping and Job Referral Decision Support System for PESO, their system focuses more on general online recruitment platforms. While their research highlights two-way recommendations between applicants and recruiters, our proposed system is designed specifically to support PESO operations by providing structured skill mapping, supervised machine learning analysis, and decision support for job referrals within a local government employment setting.Machine Learning-Based Skill Classification and Job Recommendation SystemDeveloped by Paweena Chaiaroon et al. (2025), this study presents a machine learning-based system designed to improve digital job matching in Thailand. The researchers aimed to better understand which digital skills are most in demand and how these skills relate to specific job positions. The system collected thousands of job postings from major recruitment websites and applied Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques to extract important skills from job descriptions. FastText was used/utilized to classify job titles into organized groups, then applied a Random Forest model to recommend job categories based on identified skills. Their findings showed that the Random Forest model performed better than the Decision Tree model, achieving 75% accuracy in recommending jobs across 20 digital job categories. The system was also deployed as a web-based application, allowing users to select at least four skills and receive job recommendations that match their skill profile (Chaiaroon et al., 2025). In comparison to our proposed System, their research focuses specifically on Thailand’s digital labor market and general workforce development. While the system highlights skill extraction and classification within digital job sectors, our proposed system is more localized and tailored to PESO operations, aiming to support structured skill profiling and decision-making for job referrals in a government employment setting. Both studies emphasize the importance of machine learning in improving job matching, but our system is designed to directly assist public employment services in providing more targeted and data-driven job recommendations.AI-Powered Automated Resume ScreeningDeveloped by Vernika Khatri et al. While the framework demonstrated strong performance in semantic similarity and classification tasks, the authors explicitly recommended that “Addition of a continuous learning mechanism through recruiter feedback would enable the system to learn and evolve based on actual hiring choices”. This recommendation suggests the importance of integrating adaptive learning capabilities to improve long-term matching effectiveness. Conceptual Framework	This figure shows the overall flow of the system using the Input-Process-Output model. To make everything work, the system relies on applicant records, job vacancy details, and historical placement data from PESO CSJDM, all built on top of web technologies like HTML, CSS, JavaScript, PHP, and MySQL, with RapidMiner used for developing the machine learning model. When PESO staff uploads a raw Excel file, the system automatically cleans and organizes the data, stores it in the database, and makes it ready for processing.Once a job vacancy is entered, the analytics side kicks in, it identifies the most useful applicant features through Information Gain, runs them through three machine learning algorithms being Naïve Bayes, Decision Tree, and K-Nearest Neighbors, picks the best performing one, and uses it to score and rank every applicant based on how well they fit the job. The output is a clean ranked list on the dashboard showing PESO staff exactly who among the applicants is the most qualified for the position, along with their confidence scores and profile details, making the referral process faster and far less guesswork. And as more placements get recorded and staff continue using the system, that feedback keeps feeding back into the model, making its recommendations sharper and more reliable over time.Development MethodologyThe researchers will use Rapid Application Development (RAD) as the software development methodology for building the web-based system, and the CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining) framework for developing the machine learning model.Figure 2. The evaluation aims to determine both the technical quality of the system and its acceptability among users, particularly in the context of employment facilitation and job matching.To ensure a comprehensive assessment, two evaluation models will be used, namely the ISO/IEC 25010 Software Product Quality Model and the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) Evaluation Using ISO/IEC 25010The purpose of using the ISO/IEC 25010 model is to systematically evaluate the technical performance, efficiency, and overall quality of the developed Web-Based Supervised Machine Learning–Driven Skill Mapping and Job Referral Decision Support System for PESO. This ensures that the system meets acceptable standards in delivering accurate job matching, efficient processing, and reliable service to users.The system will be evaluated based on the following seven (7) quality characteristics: Functional SuitabilityThis refers to the system’s ability to correctly perform its intended functions. For this study, it evaluates how accurately the system matches job applicants to appropriate job vacancies based on their skills, educational background, and work experience, and whether all required PESO functionalities are properly implemented.Performance Efficiency These measures how efficiently the system processes data and responds to user actions. In this system, it focuses on the speed of generating job recommendations, loading applicant data, and handling multiple users without delays.UsabilityThis refers to how easy and user-friendly the system is for both PESO staff and job applicants. It evaluates the clarity of the interface, ease of navigation, and overall user experience, especially when submitting applicant data or reviewing job matchesReliabilityThis measures the system’s ability to perform consistently under specified conditions. For this system, it evaluates whether the platform can consistently generate accurate results, avoid crashes, and maintain stable operation during continuous use.SecurityThis refers to the system’s ability to protect user data and prevent unauthorized access. In this study, it evaluates how well the system secures applicant personal information, login credentials, and job-related data, ensuring confidentiality and data integrity.MaintainabilityThese measures how easy it is to modify, update, or improve the system. For this system, it focuses on the ease of updating the machine learning model, modifying system features, and maintaining the database structure.PortabilityThis refers to the system’s ability to run across different environments. In this study, it evaluates whether the system can be accessed through different web browsers and devices without performance issues.Evaluators	The system will be evaluated by three (3) IT professionals with expertise in web development, database management, or machine learning systems.A purposive sampling method will be used to ensure that the selected evaluators have sufficient technical knowledge and experience to properly assess the system.InstrumentA structured evaluation questionnaire based on ISO/IEC 25010 quality characteristics will be used.
